Tips to land your multimedia dream internship

1. Research, research, research..

  • Do your research on a company before you apply for an internship.
  • Absorb every piece of information the company has made available on its website and blog.
  • Swott up on clients, projects, history...
  • Make a list of questions you'd ask at an interview. These questions will make sure they know your expectations !

2. Assess your finances

  • Can you afford an unpaid internship or do you need a paid internship to make things viable?
  • If the company of your dreams offers you an unpaid internship, can you scrimp, borrow and eat budget food for the duration?
  • Decide what you're prepared to sacrifice to advance your career!

3. All about timing

  • Come on board at a time that, for example, isn't bang on deadline for the company's biggest ever project.
  • Don’t get in the middle of something already big and crowded, because no one will have time to work you through a process

4. Wear the right clothes

  • Smart jeans, a smart shirt or blouse and some nice shoes for your interview, you shouldn’t go wrong with these.
  • Avoid the extremes of either smart or scruffy office wear.

5. Don't be exploited

  • Say something! Once you start your internship, if you feel as though you're being exploited, say something!
  • Remember you should be treated as an employee, not a servant.
  • Be confident and assertive and you will get more from your placement.

6. Get mentored

  • Try to find a mentor at your internship.
  • Don't be afraid to ask someone directly if they will be your mentor. Even if they say no, they may suggest someone else to approach.
  • Bondings help build your career!

7. Be part of the company

  • Approach anyone you work with - in a professional, yet friendly manner.
  • Ask what you may think are obvious questions.
  • Make sure you're learning new things, not just using skills you already possess.

8. Make time for regular meetings

  • Ask for regular meetings with different people.
  • Get feedback to get an idea of how you are doing.
  • Address any concerns you have in a more formal way.

9. Try and take away portfolio pieces

  • Bulk out your portfolio with real-world pieces, from the project you have been assigned to while you’re interning.
  • Be honest about the part you played in the project.

10. Stay in touch

  • Interns are soon forgotten - so keep in touch regularly and make sure you're not.
  • The odd email and promo mailer will certainly not hurt!

3 digital design jobs you probably didn’t know exist

1. Brand Identity Designer

The brand designer provides new and innovative ways to combine images and words to express the main message of a company. This often involves modernizing the visual image of the company in terms of logo, tagline, typography, color palette, design, etc., while remaining close to the core of the brand and what it stands for.

Brand identity is more intricate than logo creation.It is the visual element that represents how a company wants to be seen. It is the company’s visual identity or “image.” Brand identity design helps consumers remember the company and is often what brings them back.

The right brand identity design can be the company’s strongest marketing tool. It translates the values and strategic vision of a company into a consistent image that consumers become familiar with.

2. Creative/Art Director

Creative/art directors are in charge of a creative team that produces artwork to be displayed on television, billboards, the Internet, or in magazines. This job goes hand in hand with marketing.

Art directors ensure that each member of the creative team completes their work on time and to the client’s satisfaction. Most of an art director’s time is spent performing supervisory and administrative work. However, advanced training in graphic design is still required- art directors often do some of the artwork and designing themselves.

Employment opportunities for art directors are often available through advertising agencies, magazines and newspapers, public relations firms and manufacturers.

Many art directors are also self-employed or freelance. So there are both in house graphic design jobs available or they can create their own company.

3. Layout Artist

A layout artist designs the structure and layout of images and text in a pleasing format for printed media. This can include magazine work, brochures, flyers, books, CD booklets, and posters.

Layout artists: • are responsible for ensuring that images are arranged in a way that is pleasing to the eye, and directs the reader in the right flow or direction • must ensure that the typeface chosen is good for long term reading without making the eyes tired.

Layout designers are often employed at advertising agencies, magazines and newspapers, or public relations firms. There is a strong demand for in-house layout artists and freelance layout artists.
